Why this model was chosen
The Gaussian Process Regression is the scientifically optimal choice for small aerodynamic datasets with fewer than 200 simulation runs. Unlike any other model on this page, the GP does not merely predict a single number. It predicts a complete probability distribution over possible Cd values, meaning every prediction comes with a calibrated confidence interval. In regions of the parameter space that are densely covered by training data, the GP uncertainty collapses to near zero. In regions with sparse coverage, the uncertainty inflates — acting as a direct signal that a CFD simulation should be run at exactly those conditions. This property is the foundation of Bayesian optimisation: the next simulation is always placed at the point of maximum expected information gain, rather than chosen arbitrarily. The model uses a Matérn covariance kernel with smoothness parameter ν = 2.5, which assumes the drag function is twice differentiable but not infinitely smooth. This is physically appropriate for aerodynamic quantities near flow separation phenomena such as the rear slant critical angle and the yaw-induced vortex shedding regime.
Sample size requirements
355 samples (WindsorML) — optimal GP range 100–200 — optimal for GP 300+ — diminishing returns vs. Neural Net
Technical configuration
Kernel: ConstantKernel(1.0) × Matérn(ν=2.5, ℓ=1.0) Normalise outputs: True · Alpha: 1×10⁻⁶ Optimiser restarts: 10 · Scaling: StandardScaler Uncertainty output: mean ± 1σ at every query point
Accuracy vs. other models
At 50 training samples, the GP achieves the lowest cross-validated RMSE of 0.00624 — 35% better than Gradient Boosting (0.00960) and 44% better than Random Forest (0.01110). The Neural Network models are statistically underpowered at this sample size and produce unreliable validation metrics. As sample count grows beyond 300, Neural Network and XGBoost models will likely surpass GP performance, as GPs scale cubically with training size and become computationally expensive to train.

Why this model was chosen
Gradient Boosting builds an ensemble by training trees sequentially — each tree fits the residuals of all previous trees. A deliberately low learning rate of 0.05 forces the model to take many small correction steps rather than large ones, which prevents overfitting even on the current 50-sample dataset. Shallow trees with maximum depth of 3 capture the dominant nonlinear relationships — such as the drag peak near 30–35° rear slant angle and the rapid drag increase above 8° yaw — without memorising noise in the training data. Gradient Boosting is considered the most production-ready model because it produces consistent, interpretable predictions with no stochastic component. When a single definitive Cd estimate is required — for a design review, for a management presentation, or for a comparative ranking of design candidates — Gradient Boosting is the recommended output to use.

Accuracy vs. other models
At the current sample size, Gradient Boosting ranks second with a cross-validated RMSE of 0.00960, which falls within the "engineering grade" band (below 0.010). Training R² of 0.990 indicates the model generalises well without severe overfitting. It significantly outperforms Random Forest (0.01110) by learning from ordered residual corrections rather than independent tree averaging. The GP model outperforms it primarily in uncertainty quantification; for raw point-prediction accuracy the gap between the two models is modest.

Why this model was chosen
The Random Forest's most important contribution is not its drag prediction — Gradient Boosting and the Gaussian Process both outperform it on that task. Its value lies in the Gini-based feature importance scores generated as a byproduct of training. These scores answer the question that matters most before planning the next batch of CFD simulations: which parameter should be varied in the next design of experiments to maximise information gain? Your current results show that yaw angle explains 71.4% of total Cd variation, rear slant angle 13.8%, and spoiler angle 8.7%. This means that when planning new simulations, the priority order is unambiguous: vary yaw angle most densely, then rear slant, then spoiler. Ground clearance and frontal area together account for less than 6% and need only sparse coverage. This kind of evidence-based simulation planning is impossible without a model like Random Forest.

Accuracy vs. other models
The Random Forest ranks third in predictive accuracy with a CV RMSE of 0.01110. This sits in the "acceptable for early screening" band but not in engineering-grade territory. The reason it underperforms Gradient Boosting is structural: Random Forest trees are trained independently and their predictions averaged, whereas Gradient Boosting trees are trained sequentially to correct each other's errors. For small datasets this sequential correction provides a significant advantage. At 200+ samples, the gap between Random Forest and Gradient Boosting narrows considerably.

Why this model was chosen
The Multi-Layer Perceptron is included in the suite specifically to demonstrate the sample-size dependency of neural network methods and to provide a ready-to-activate model once the WindsorML dataset (355 simulations) is incorporated. Unlike tree-based models, the MLP learns a continuous global function across all inputs simultaneously, which allows it to capture subtle interaction effects between parameters — for example, the combined effect of high yaw angle and a steep rear slant, which tree-based models can only approximate through step-function boundaries. Early stopping with a patience of 30 epochs automatically prevents overfitting without requiring manual regularisation tuning.

Accuracy vs. other models
At 50 training samples the MLP ranks fourth with CV RMSE of approximately 0.035 and validation R² of −0.25 on WindsorML data. The negative R² indicates the model is performing worse than a simple mean baseline — a clear sign of insufficient training data. This is expected and not a fault of the architecture. With 355 WindsorML samples, the MLP is projected to achieve RMSE in the 0.015–0.020 range, competitive with the GP at small scale. With 500 DrivAerML samples and architecture tuning, it should reach 0.010–0.012, approaching Gradient Boosting performance.
Custom Built Model — MRV-Aero-Stack v1
Featured · Production

A purpose-built stacked ensemble that combines five heterogeneous base learners — XGBoost, LightGBM, CatBoost, ExtraTrees and a Matérn Gaussian Process — with hill-climb weight optimisation on out-of-fold predictions. Adds physics-informed feature engineering (pairwise interactions, log/sqrt transforms, dynamic-pressure and Reynolds proxies) and a log-Cd target transform to stabilise variance. Achieves a 17.6% lower CV RMSE than the best single-model baseline. Uncertainty is provided as the standard deviation across the five base learners.

Why a stacked ensemble
No single model class is best on every region of the Cd surface. Tree ensembles capture sharp nonlinearities (flow-separation cliffs around critical rear angles); Gaussian Processes capture the smooth interior of the design space with calibrated uncertainty. Stacking lets each learner contribute where it is strongest, and hill-climb weight optimisation on out-of-fold predictions finds the combination that minimises real (held-out) error — not training error. This follows the Kaggle-grandmaster ensemble recipe (NVIDIA, 2025) and matches the consensus in the tabular-ML literature (Shwartz-Ziv & Armon, 2021): trees plus a smooth-function model outperform any single architecture at sample sizes below ~1000.
Technical configuration
Base learners (5): XGBoost, LightGBM, CatBoost, ExtraTrees, GP (Matérn ν=2.5 + WhiteKernel) Feature engineering: pairwise products, x², log(x), √x, dynamic-pressure q = ½ρV², Reynolds proxy Target transform: log(Cd) — stabilises variance across the 0.31-0.44 range Out-of-fold: 5-fold CV, 3-seed bagging per base learner (variance reduction) Meta-combiner: hill-climb vs positive-Ridge — picked per-dataset by inner CV Uncertainty: cross-model std (epistemic) + GP predictive σ (calibrated)
How it compares to the four baselines
On the 60-sample Mahindra dataset the CV ranking is: Custom Stack 0.00779 > GP 0.00946 > RF 0.01418 > GB 0.01624 > NN 0.11715. The Custom model beats the best single baseline (GP) by 17.6% RMSE and 13.3% MAE. The hill-climb weights are dataset-adaptive — on this small, smooth dataset the weights concentrate on GP + ExtraTrees; on the WindsorML 355-sample benchmark they shift toward XGBoost + LightGBM, because gradient-boosted trees scale better than GPs past ~200 points. That adaptivity is the point of stacking.

Why this approach reduces CFD simulation count
Traditional aerodynamic design requires running a CFD simulation for every design variant, a process that takes 6 to 48 hours per run on a high-performance cluster. With 20 design parameters and 10 levels each, a full factorial design would require 10 to the power of 20 simulations, which is computationally impossible over any engineering timescale. The surrogate model approach breaks this barrier. The 355 WindsorML high-fidelity WMLES runs are used to train a mathematical model that approximates the full CFD solver. Once trained, the surrogate predicts Cd in under one millisecond for any parameter combination, enabling rapid screening of thousands of Windsor body variants. The Gaussian Process additionally provides uncertainty estimates, enabling active learning: the next CFD simulation is always run at the point of highest model uncertainty, maximising information gain per run. This strategy is known as Bayesian optimisation and can reduce the simulation count required to find an optimal design by five to ten times compared to random or grid sampling. At a practical level, this translates to faster design cycles, reduced HPC costs, and the ability to explore more creative design variants early in the development process, before clay modelling or wind tunnel testing are committed to.
WindsorML Parameter Definitions
ratio_length_back_fast (rlbf) — Dimensionless ratio of the rear fastback length to the total body length. Lower values represent a steeper, more abrupt rear end; higher values represent a longer, more gradual taper.

ratio_height_nose_windshield (rhnw) — Height ratio between the nose leading edge and the windshield base. Controls how bluff the front face is and how aggressively the flow transitions to the windshield.

ratio_height_fast_back (rhfb) — Height ratio of the fastback surface relative to the body. Governs the severity of the rear slope and the onset of trailing-edge separation.

side_taper — Side wall taper angle in degrees. Positive taper narrows the body toward the rear, reducing the separated wake width.

clearance — Ground clearance in millimetres. Affects the underbody flow velocity and the balance between base pressure and underbody suction.

bottom_taper_angle — Angle of the underbody diffuser ramp in degrees. Higher angles increase underbody pressure recovery but risk diffuser stall above approximately 12 degrees.

frontal_area — Projected frontal area in square metres at Windsor body scale. Directly scales the pressure drag component via Cd = Fd / (0.5 * rho * V2 * A).
