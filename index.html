ML Models and Methodology
Five Models, Five Purposes
Each model in this suite was selected for a distinct engineering or statistical reason. All five are trained on the same 355 WindsorML Wall Modelled LES simulations and evaluated with identical 5 fold cross validation. Expand any card using the See more button to read the full technical rationale, sample size guidance, configuration details, and WindsorML accuracy results.

A probabilistic model that predicts a full probability distribution over Cd rather than a single point value. Every prediction carries a calibrated confidence interval, making it the only model in this suite that can quantify how uncertain it is about any given Windsor geometry configuration. This uncertainty output is the foundation of Bayesian optimisation for targeted CFD run placement.

Why this model was chosen
The Gaussian Process is the statistically optimal choice for the 355 sample WindsorML dataset. Unlike every other model in this suite, it does not predict a single number but a complete probability distribution over possible Cd values. Every prediction carries a calibrated confidence interval. In regions of the Windsor parameter space that are densely sampled by the 355 WMLES runs, GP uncertainty collapses to near zero. In sparse regions, uncertainty inflates, acting as a direct signal that a new simulation should be placed there. This is the foundation of Bayesian optimisation: the next CFD run is always placed at the point of maximum expected information gain rather than chosen arbitrarily. The model uses a Matern covariance kernel with smoothness parameter 2.5, which assumes the drag surface is twice differentiable but not infinitely smooth. This is physically appropriate for Windsor body aerodynamics near the critical fastback separation phenomena where Cd changes sharply as rear geometry crosses separation thresholds.

Accuracy on WindsorML
On the 355 sample WindsorML dataset, the GP achieves CV RMSE of 0.00946, which places it firmly in the engineering grade band suitable for design screening decisions. It outperforms Gradient Boosting by 42% and Random Forest by 33% in RMSE. The Neural Network is now competitive at 355 samples but still trails the GP. Above approximately 1000 samples, the GP cubic scaling cost would make it impractical to retrain, at which point the MLP and gradient boosted trees would become the preferred alternatives for production inference.

Why this model was chosen
Gradient Boosting builds its ensemble by training trees sequentially, with each tree fitting the residuals of all previous trees in the sequence. A learning rate of 0.05 forces many small correction steps rather than large ones, which prevents overfitting across the 355 sample WindsorML dataset. Shallow trees with maximum depth of 3 capture dominant nonlinear relationships in the Windsor geometry space, such as the drag sensitivity to rear fastback ratio in the 0.30 to 0.45 range, without memorising noise from individual simulation runs. Gradient Boosting is the recommended model when a single definitive Cd estimate is needed for a design review, a management presentation, or a ranked comparison of Windsor body variants, because it produces consistent and deterministic predictions with no stochastic component whatsoever. There is no seed variability or sampling randomness in its predictions.

Why this model was chosen
The Random Forest's most important contribution to this suite is not drag prediction accuracy but the Gini based feature importance scores it generates as a byproduct of training. On the 355 sample WindsorML dataset, these scores reveal that rear fastback ratio accounts for 34.9% of total Cd variation, nose to windshield height ratio accounts for 19.8%, and fastback height ratio accounts for 17.8%. Custom Built Model — MRV Aero Stack v1
Featured and Production

A purpose built stacked ensemble combining five heterogeneous base learners: XGBoost, LightGBM, CatBoost, ExtraTrees, and a Matern Gaussian Process. Uses hill climb weight optimisation on out of fold predictions to find the combination that minimises real held out error. Adds physics informed feature engineering including pairwise interactions, squared terms, log and square root transforms, and a dynamic pressure proxy. Uses a log Cd target transform to stabilise variance. Achieves 17.6% lower CV RMSE than the best single model baseline on the 355 sample WindsorML dataset.

Why a stacked ensemble
No single model class is optimal across every region of the Windsor Cd surface. Tree ensembles capture sharp nonlinearities near critical fastback separation thresholds where Cd changes rapidly with small geometry adjustments. The Gaussian Process captures the smooth interior of the design space with calibrated uncertainty. CatBoost handles ordered boosting with different bias properties than XGBoost and LightGBM. ExtraTrees provides variance reduction through extreme randomisation. Stacking lets each learner contribute where it is strongest. Hill climb weight optimisation on out of fold predictions finds the combination that minimises real held out error rather than training error, which prevents the meta learner from overfitting to the base learner training predictions.
Ensemble weight transparency
At 355 samples the stack is GP dominant with weights of GP 0.969 and ExtraTrees 0.031. XGBoost, LightGBM, and CatBoost receive zero weight because the GP dominates at this sample size and the tree boosters provide no additional information that improves out of fold performance. This is expected and not a flaw in the architecture. As the dataset grows toward 1000 or more samples from DrivAerML, the hill climb weights will redistribute meaningfully toward XGBoost and LightGBM because gradient boosted trees scale better than GP past approximately 300 to 400 training points where GP matrix inversion overhead begins to dominate.
Technical configuration
Base learners (5): XGBoost, LightGBM, CatBoost, ExtraTrees, GP (Matern nu=2.5 + WhiteKernel) Feature engineering: pairwise products, squared terms, log(x), sqrt(x), dynamic pressure q = 0.5 times rho times V squared Target transform: log(Cd) stabilises variance across the Windsor Cd range Out of fold training: 5 fold CV with 3 seed bagging per base learner Meta combiner: hill climb versus positive Ridge, winner selected by inner CV Uncertainty: cross model standard deviation (epistemic) + GP predictive sigma (calibrated)
Results on WindsorML
On the 355 sample WindsorML dataset, the CV ranking is Custom Stack 0.00779, GP 0.00946, RF 0.01418, GB 0.01624, MLP 0.01850. The Custom Stack beats the best single model baseline by 17.6% in RMSE and 13.3% in MAE. These improvements are real out of fold improvements, not training set overfitting, because the hill climb weights are optimised exclusively on out of fold predictions from the base learners. At 500 or more DrivAerML samples, the weights will shift toward XGBoost and LightGBM and the improvement margin over GP alone is expected to widen substantially.

WindsorML

355 high fidelity Wall Modelled LES simulations performed on the Windsor body across a Latin hypercube sampled design space. Available on HuggingFace under CC BY SA. Each simulation used more than 280 million mesh cells to ensure the highest possible accuracy and correlation to experimental data from the 4th AutoCFD workshop. DrivAerML

500 parametrically morphed variants of the DrivAer notchback generic vehicle, a realistic production car geometry used by major OEMs for aerodynamic research. Uses industrial grade hybrid RANS LES scale resolving CFD with automated meshing workflows producing grids of approximately 160 million cells per simulation. Provides the best geometric match to real production sedan bodies of any publicly available CFD dataset and is available on HuggingFace under CC BY SA.

Why this approach reduces CFD simulation count
Traditional aerodynamic design requires running a full CFD simulation for every design variant considered, a process that takes between 6 and 48 hours per run on a high performance computing cluster. At that rate, exploring even 100 Windsor body variants would consume months of compute time and significant cost. The surrogate model approach breaks this barrier entirely. The 355 WindsorML high fidelity WMLES runs are used once to train a mathematical model that approximates the full CFD solver across the entire Windsor parameter space. Once trained, the surrogate predicts Cd in under one millisecond for any Windsor parameter combination, enabling rapid screening of thousands of design variants at negligible computational cost. The Gaussian Process additionally provides calibrated uncertainty estimates that enable active learning, where the next CFD simulation is always placed at the point of highest model uncertainty rather than chosen arbitrarily. This Bayesian optimisation strategy can reduce the total simulation count required to find an optimal design by five to ten times compared to random or grid sampling strategies, making high fidelity aerodynamic surrogate modelling accessible to teams without access to unlimited HPC resources.
WindsorML Parameter Definitions
The 7 dimensionless Windsor body geometry parameters used as model inputs across all five surrogate models
ratio_length_back_fast (rlbf)
Dimensionless ratio of the rear fastback length to the total body length. Lower values produce a steeper and more abrupt rear end with earlier flow separation. Higher values create a longer and more gradual taper that delays separation, generally reducing drag at moderate values before increasing it again at extremes.
ratio_height_nose_windshield (rhnw)
Height ratio between the nose leading edge and the windshield base. Controls how bluff the front face appears to the oncoming flow and how aggressively the streamlines transition onto the windshield plane. Lower values produce a more gradual front transition while higher values produce a more abrupt step at the windshield base.
ratio_height_fast_back (rhfb)
Height ratio of the fastback surface relative to the overall body height. Governs the severity of the rear slope angle and the onset of trailing edge separation from the roof surface. This parameter interacts strongly with rlbf because both govern the rear geometry that determines wake structure and base pressure coefficient.
side_taper
Side wall taper angle in degrees. Positive taper progressively narrows the Windsor body toward the rear, reducing the width of the separated wake region and typically lowering total drag. Excessive taper can induce asymmetric separation that reverses the drag benefit above approximately 12 to 15 degrees depending on rear geometry.
clearance
Ground clearance in millimetres between the lowest body surface and the ground plane. Affects underbody flow velocity and the balance between base pressure recovery and underbody suction force. Lower clearance accelerates underbody flow, increasing the Venturi effect, while higher clearance reduces it. The optimal clearance depends strongly on the underbody diffuser angle.
bottom_taper_angle
Angle of the underbody diffuser ramp in degrees. Higher angles increase underbody pressure recovery by decelerating the accelerated underbody flow more aggressively, but risk diffuser stall and catastrophic flow separation above approximately 12 degrees. Diffuser stall reverses the drag benefit entirely and can increase drag above the baseline squareback configuration.
frontal_area (m squared)
Projected frontal area in square metres at Windsor body scale. Directly scales the pressure drag component because the drag force Fd equals Cd times 0.5 times air density times velocity squared times frontal area A. Windsor body values in the dataset range from approximately 0.080 to 0.120 metres squared. Changes in frontal area that alter body proportions also indirectly affect the flow field and therefore Cd itself, making this an interaction variable with the geometry ratios.
