A probabilistic model that predicts a full probability distribution over Cd rather than a single value, giving both a mean prediction and a calibrated confidence interval at every point in the Windsor geometry parameter space.

Why this model was chosen
The Gaussian Process is the statistically optimal choice for the 355-sample WindsorML dataset. Unlike any other model in this suite, it does not predict a single number but a complete probability distribution over possible Cd values. Every prediction carries a calibrated confidence interval. In regions of the Windsor parameter space that are densely sampled by the 355 WMLES runs, GP uncertainty collapses to near zero. In sparse regions, uncertainty inflates, acting as a direct signal that a new simulation should be placed there. This is the foundation of Bayesian optimisation: the next CFD run is always placed at the point of maximum expected information gain rather than chosen arbitrarily. The model uses a Matern covariance kernel with smoothness parameter 2.5, which assumes the drag surface is twice differentiable but not infinitely smooth. This is physically appropriate for Windsor body aerodynamics near the critical fastback separation phenomena.

Accuracy on WindsorML
On the 355-sample WindsorML dataset, the GP achieves CV RMSE of 0.00946, which places it firmly in the engineering-grade band. It outperforms Gradient Boosting by 42% and Random Forest by 33% in RMSE. The Neural Network is now competitive at 355 samples but still trails the GP. Above approximately 1000 samples, the GP cubic scaling cost would make it impractical to retrain, at which point the MLP and gradient-boosted trees would become preferred.

Why this model was chosen
Gradient Boosting builds its ensemble by training trees sequentially, with each tree fitting the residuals of all previous trees. A learning rate of 0.05 forces many small correction steps rather than large ones, preventing overfitting across the 355-sample WindsorML dataset. Shallow trees with maximum depth of 3 capture dominant nonlinear relationships in the Windsor geometry space, such as the drag sensitivity to rear fastback ratio in the 0.30 to 0.45 range, without memorising noise. Gradient Boosting is the recommended model when a single definitive Cd estimate is needed for a design review, a management presentation, or a ranked comparison of Windsor body variants, because it produces consistent, deterministic predictions with no stochastic component.

Why this model was chosen
The Random Forest's most important contribution is not drag prediction accuracy but the Gini-based feature importance scores it generates as a byproduct of training. On the 355-sample WindsorML dataset, these scores show that rear fastback ratio (rlbf) accounts for 34.9% of total Cd variation, nose to windshield height ratio (rhnw) accounts for 19.8%, and fastback height ratio (rhfb) accounts for 17.8%. This means that when planning the next batch of Windsor simulations, the priority order for parameter variation is clear: concentrate new runs at varying rlbf first, then rhnw, then rhfb. Side taper, ground clearance, bottom taper angle, and frontal area together account for under 30% and need only moderate coverage in a follow-on design of experiments.

Why this model
The sklearn MLPRegressor (Input 6 to 128 to 64 to 32 to Output 1) is now trained on the full 355-sample WindsorML dataset, which brings it into a functional operating regime. Unlike tree-based models, the MLP learns a continuous global function across all 7 Windsor geometry parameters simultaneously. This allows it to capture smooth interaction effects between parameters, for example the combined influence of rear fastback ratio and fastback height ratio on trailing edge separation, which tree-based models can only approximate through axis-aligned step boundaries. Early stopping with a patience of 30 epochs prevents overfitting without manual regularisation tuning. With 11,040 trainable weights across its three hidden layers, the MLP has approximately 32 weights per training sample at 355 samples, which is adequate but not ideal. Performance will improve meaningfully with DrivAerML at 500 samples.

With 355 WindsorML samples, the MLP achieves CV RMSE of approximately 0.0185 and R squared of 0.71. It now outperforms Gradient Boosting in R squared and is competitive with Random Forest in RMSE. Custom Built Model — MRV-Aero-Stack v1
Featured · Production

A purpose-built stacked ensemble combining five heterogeneous base learners — XGBoost, LightGBM, CatBoost, ExtraTrees and a Matern Gaussian Process — with hill-climb weight optimisation on out-of-fold predictions. Adds physics-informed feature engineering and a log-Cd target transform. Achieves 17.6% lower CV RMSE than the best single-model baseline on the WindsorML dataset.

Architecture
5-model stack
Why a stacked ensemble
No single model class is optimal across every region of the Windsor Cd surface. Tree ensembles capture sharp nonlinearities near critical fastback separation thresholds; the Gaussian Process captures the smooth interior of the design space with calibrated uncertainty. Stacking lets each learner contribute where it is strongest. Hill-climb weight optimisation on out-of-fold predictions finds the combination that minimises real held-out error rather than training error.
Technical configuration
Base learners (5): XGBoost, LightGBM, CatBoost, ExtraTrees, GP (Matern v=2.5 + WhiteKernel) Feature engineering: pairwise products, x^2, log(x), sqrt(x), dynamic-pressure q = 0.5*rho*V^2 Target transform: log(Cd) stabilises variance across the Windsor Cd range Out-of-fold: 5-fold CV, 3-seed bagging per base learner Meta-combiner: hill-climb vs positive-Ridge, winner picked by inner CV Uncertainty: cross-model std (epistemic) + GP predictive sigma (calibrated)
How it compares on WindsorML
On the 355-sample WindsorML dataset, the CV ranking is: Custom Stack 0.00779, GP 0.00946, RF 0.01418, GB 0.01624, MLP 0.01850. The Custom Stack beats the best single baseline by 17.6% in RMSE and 13.3% in MAE. The hill-climb weights concentrate on GP and ExtraTrees at this sample count. At 500 or more DrivAerML samples, the weights will shift toward XGBoost and LightGBM because gradient-boosted trees scale better than GP past approximately 300 to 400 points.
Ensemble weight transparency
The hill-climb optimiser assigned weights: GP 0.969 · ExtraTrees 0.031 · XGB/LGB/CatBoost 0.000. At 355 samples the stack is effectively GP-dominant — the XGBoost, LightGBM and CatBoost learners receive zero weight because the GP dominates at this sample size. The stacking architecture is designed to adapt: as the dataset grows toward 1000+ samples these weights will redistribute toward the gradient-boosted trees.

Why this approach reduces CFD simulation count
Traditional aerodynamic design requires running a CFD simulation for every design variant, a process that takes 6 to 48 hours per run on a high-performance cluster. The surrogate model approach breaks this barrier. The 355 WindsorML high-fidelity WMLES runs train a mathematical model that approximates the full CFD solver. Once trained, the surrogate predicts Cd in under one millisecond for any Windsor parameter combination, enabling rapid screening of thousands of design variants. The Gaussian Process additionally provides uncertainty estimates enabling active learning, where the next CFD simulation is always placed at the point of highest model uncertainty. This Bayesian optimisation strategy can reduce the simulation count required to find an optimal design by five to ten times compared to random or grid sampling.
WindsorML Parameter Definitions
The 7 dimensionless Windsor body geometry parameters used as model inputs
ratio_length_back_fast (rlbf)
Dimensionless ratio of the rear fastback length to the total body length. Lower values produce a steeper, more abrupt rear end with earlier separation. Higher values create a longer, more gradual taper with delayed separation.
ratio_height_nose_windshield (rhnw)
Height ratio between the nose leading edge and the windshield base. Controls how bluff the front face appears to the oncoming flow and how aggressively the streamlines transition to the windshield plane.
ratio_height_fast_back (rhfb)
Height ratio of the fastback surface relative to the overall body height. Governs the severity of the rear slope angle and the onset of trailing-edge separation from the roof surface.
side_taper
Side wall taper angle in degrees. Positive taper narrows the Windsor body toward the rear, reducing the width of the separated wake and typically lowering total drag.
clearance
Ground clearance in millimetres between the lowest body surface and the ground plane. Affects underbody flow velocity and the balance between base pressure recovery and underbody suction force.
bottom_taper_angle
Angle of the underbody diffuser ramp in degrees. Higher angles increase underbody pressure recovery but risk diffuser stall and flow separation above approximately 12 degrees, which reverses the drag benefit.
frontal_area
Projected frontal area in square metres at Windsor body scale. Directly scales the pressure drag component: Cd = Fd divided by (0.5 times rho times V squared times A). Windsor body values range from approximately 0.080 to 0.120 m squared.
