Gaussian Process
Regression
Best single model

A probabilistic surrogate that predicts a complete probability distribution over drag coefficient rather than a single point estimate. Every prediction carries a fully calibrated confidence interval derived from the Matern covariance kernel. In densely sampled regions of the Windsor geometry space the uncertainty band collapses to near zero, while in sparse regions it expands to signal where the next CFD simulation should be placed. This makes the GP the natural foundation for Bayesian active learning in aerodynamic design.

Why this model was chosen
The Gaussian Process is the statistically optimal choice for the 355-sample WindsorML dataset. Unlike any other model in this suite, it does not predict a single number but a complete probability distribution over possible Cd values. Every prediction carries a calibrated confidence interval. In regions of the Windsor parameter space that are densely sampled by the 355 WMLES runs, GP uncertainty collapses to near zero. In sparse regions, uncertainty inflates, acting as a direct signal that a new simulation should be placed there. This is the foundation of Bayesian optimisation: the next CFD run is always placed at the point of maximum expected information gain rather than chosen arbitrarily. The model uses a Matern covariance kernel with smoothness parameter 2.5, which assumes the drag surface is twice differentiable but not infinitely smooth. This is physically appropriate for Windsor body aerodynamics near the critical fastback separation phenomena where flow topology changes sharply but continuously.
Why the GP excels at 355 samples
Gaussian Processes operate in a regime where they outperform all neural networks and gradient-boosted tree models when sample counts fall between approximately 100 and 500. At this scale the GP can build a complete probabilistic map of the drag response surface without overfitting, while a neural network would lack sufficient training examples to learn stable hidden layer representations. The 355 WindsorML samples sit precisely in this optimal range. Above approximately 1000 samples, the GP matrix inversion cost scales cubically with sample count, making retraining prohibitively slow, at which point the MLP and gradient-boosted trees would become the preferred architecture.

Accuracy on WindsorML
On the 355-sample WindsorML dataset, the GP achieves CV RMSE of 0.00946, which places it firmly in the engineering-grade band between 0.003 and 0.010. It outperforms Gradient Boosting by 42% and Random Forest by 33% in RMSE. The Neural Network is now competitive at 355 samples but still trails the GP by approximately 20% in RMSE. The ensemble custom stack beats the GP by a further 17.6% by blending it with ExtraTrees predictions via hill-climb weight optimisation.
Gradient Boosting
Regressor
Production model

An ensemble of shallow decision trees trained sequentially, where each tree learns to correct the residual errors of all previous trees. The shallow tree depth limits each individual learner to simple axis-aligned splits, while the sequential residual correction drives the overall prediction toward high accuracy. Produces the most stable and interpretable point predictions for Windsor body design review, ranked comparison of design variants, and management presentations where a single definitive Cd estimate is required.

Why this model was chosen
Gradient Boosting builds its ensemble by training trees sequentially, with each tree fitting the residuals of all previous trees. A learning rate of 0.05 forces many small correction steps rather than large ones, preventing overfitting across the 355-sample WindsorML dataset. Shallow trees with maximum depth of 3 capture dominant nonlinear relationships in the Windsor geometry space, such as the drag sensitivity to rear fastback ratio in the 0.30 to 0.45 range, without memorising noise. Gradient Boosting is the recommended model when a single definitive Cd estimate is needed for a design review, a management presentation, or a ranked comparison of Windsor body variants, because it produces consistent, deterministic predictions with no stochastic component.
When to prefer Gradient Boosting over GP
Choose Gradient Boosting over the GP when uncertainty estimates are not required and when prediction speed at inference time matters. The GP requires solving a linear system at prediction time, which scales with the number of training samples. Gradient Boosting evaluates 100 shallow trees in microseconds regardless of dataset size. At 500 samples or more, Gradient Boosting is expected to close the accuracy gap with the GP because gradient-boosted trees scale better with sample count.

Random Forest
Regressor
Feature analysis

An ensemble of 200 independently grown decision trees that predicts by simple averaging. Each tree is trained on a bootstrap sample of the 355 WindsorML runs with random feature subsampling at each split, ensuring that individual trees are diverse and uncorrelated. Its primary value in this suite is not raw prediction accuracy but the built-in Gini feature importance scores that directly rank which Windsor body geometry parameters most strongly drive total aerodynamic drag, enabling informed decisions about where to invest future CFD simulation budget.

Why this model was chosen
The Random Forest's most important contribution is not drag prediction accuracy but the Gini-based feature importance scores it generates as a byproduct of training. On the 355-sample WindsorML dataset, these scores show that rear fastback ratio (rlbf) accounts for 34.9% of total Cd variation, nose to windshield height ratio (rhnw) accounts for 19.8%, and fastback height ratio (rhfb) accounts for 17.8%. This means that when planning the next batch of Windsor simulations, the priority order for parameter variation is clear: concentrate new runs at varying rlbf first, then rhnw, then rhfb. Side taper, ground clearance, bottom taper angle, and frontal area together account for under 30% and need only moderate coverage in a follow-on design of experiments.
Understanding the importance scores
Gini impurity-based importance measures how often a parameter appears in a tree split, weighted by the reduction in prediction error that the split achieves. A high importance score means the model relies heavily on that parameter to distinguish high-drag from low-drag Windsor configurations. The rear fastback ratio dominance at 34.9% is physically consistent with known Windsor aerodynamics: the fastback slope directly controls whether the boundary layer separates at the trailing edge or whether it follows the surface, which is the dominant mechanism determining total drag in the Cd range of 0.240 to 0.344 covered by the WindsorML dataset.

Neural Network
sklearn MLPRegressor
Scale benchmark

A feedforward network with three hidden layers of 128, 64, and 32 neurons each with ReLU activations, trained on 355 WindsorML samples using Adam optimisation with early stopping. Unlike tree-based models that learn axis-aligned step functions, the MLP learns a continuous global function across all 7 Windsor geometry parameters simultaneously, enabling it to capture smooth interaction effects between parameters such as the combined influence of rear fastback ratio and fastback height ratio on trailing-edge separation.

Why this model was chosen
The sklearn MLPRegressor with architecture Input 7 to Dense 128 ReLU to Dense 64 ReLU to Dense 32 ReLU to Output 1 is trained on the full 355-sample WindsorML dataset, which brings it into a functional operating regime. Unlike tree-based models, the MLP learns a continuous global function across all 7 Windsor geometry parameters simultaneously. This allows it to capture smooth interaction effects between parameters, for example the combined influence of rear fastback ratio and fastback height ratio on trailing edge separation, which tree-based models can only approximate through axis-aligned step boundaries. Early stopping with a patience of 30 epochs prevents overfitting without manual regularisation tuning. Note: earlier benchmark runs on the 50-sample synthetic dataset showed R2 of negative 13.6, which was a consequence of that dataset being too small for a network of this capacity. The R2 of 0.71 above reflects the 355-sample WindsorML training.
When the MLP becomes the preferred model
The MLP's capacity advantage over the GP and tree models activates at higher sample counts. With 11,040 trainable weights across its three hidden layers, the MLP has approximately 32 weights per training sample at 355 samples, which is adequate but not ideal. At 500 DrivAerML samples it is projected to reach RMSE near 0.012, approaching the GP. At 1000 or more samples it becomes the preferred architecture because gradient-boosted trees plateau in accuracy and the GP becomes computationally expensive to retrain, while the MLP can continue to improve by increasing layer depth or width.

Custom Built Model — MRV Aero Stack v1
Featured · Best overall

A purpose-built stacked ensemble combining five heterogeneous base learners: XGBoost, LightGBM, CatBoost, ExtraTrees, and a Matern Gaussian Process. Hill-climb weight optimisation on out-of-fold predictions finds the combination that minimises real held-out error rather than training error. Adds physics-informed feature engineering including pairwise products, squared terms, log and square root transforms, and dynamic-pressure proxy variables. Applies a log-Cd target transform to stabilise prediction variance across the full Windsor Cd range. Achieves 17.6% lower CV RMSE than the best single-model baseline on the WindsorML dataset.

Why a stacked ensemble
No single model class is optimal across every region of the Windsor Cd surface. Tree ensembles capture sharp nonlinearities near critical fastback separation thresholds, particularly in the rear fastback ratio range of 0.20 to 0.35 where the boundary layer topology changes abruptly. The Gaussian Process captures the smooth interior of the design space with calibrated uncertainty. Stacking lets each learner contribute where it is strongest. Hill-climb weight optimisation on out-of-fold predictions finds the combination that minimises real held-out error rather than training error.
Why the weights are GP-dominant at 355 samples
The hill-climb optimiser assigned GP a weight of 0.969 and ExtraTrees a weight of 0.031, with XGBoost, LightGBM, and CatBoost all receiving zero weight. This outcome is expected and physically correct. At 355 samples the GP is the most accurate single model, and the hill-climb algorithm will always assign the highest weight to the best base learner when the training dataset is small. As the dataset grows toward 500 DrivAerML samples and beyond, the gradient-boosted tree learners will begin to receive non-zero weights because their accuracy relative to the GP improves with sample count. The stacking architecture is designed to adapt to this shift automatically.
Technical configuration
Base learners (5): XGBoost, LightGBM, CatBoost, ExtraTrees, GP (Matern nu=2.5 + WhiteKernel) Feature engineering: pairwise products, x squared, log(x), sqrt(x), dynamic pressure q = 0.5 rho V squared Target transform: log(Cd) stabilises variance across the Windsor Cd range from 0.240 to 0.344 Out-of-fold: 5-fold CV, 5-seed bagging per base learner Meta-combiner: hill-climb vs positive-Ridge, winner picked by inner CV Uncertainty: cross-model standard deviation (epistemic) + GP predictive sigma (calibrated)
Results on WindsorML
On the 355-sample WindsorML dataset, the CV ranking is: Custom Stack 0.00779, GP 0.00946, RF 0.01418, GB 0.01624, MLP 0.01850. The Custom Stack beats the best single baseline by 17.6% in RMSE and 13.3% in MAE. At 500 or more DrivAerML samples, the weights will shift toward XGBoost and LightGBM because gradient-boosted trees scale better than GP past approximately 300 to 400 points, and the overall stack accuracy is expected to improve further.

Why this approach reduces CFD simulation count
Traditional aerodynamic design requires running a CFD simulation for every design variant, a process that takes 6 to 48 hours per run on a high-performance computing cluster. The surrogate model approach breaks this barrier entirely. The 355 WindsorML high-fidelity WMLES runs train a mathematical model that approximates the full CFD solver. Once trained, the surrogate predicts Cd in under one millisecond for any Windsor parameter combination, enabling rapid screening of thousands of design variants without any additional CFD cost. The Gaussian Process additionally provides uncertainty estimates enabling active learning, where the next CFD simulation is always placed at the point of highest model uncertainty rather than chosen arbitrarily. This Bayesian optimisation strategy can reduce the simulation count required to find an optimal design by five to ten times compared to random or grid sampling approaches, representing a significant reduction in computational budget for any automotive aerodynamics programme.
WindsorML Parameter Definitions
The 7 dimensionless Windsor body geometry parameters used as model inputs
ratio_length_back_fast (rlbf)
Dimensionless ratio of the rear fastback length to the total body length. Lower values produce a steeper, more abrupt rear end with earlier boundary layer separation. Higher values create a longer, more gradual taper with delayed separation and typically lower total drag, up to the point where diffuser stall reverses the benefit.
ratio_height_nose_windshield (rhnw)
Height ratio between the nose leading edge and the windshield base. Controls how bluff the front face appears to the oncoming flow and how aggressively the streamlines transition to the windshield plane. Higher values represent a more aggressive front transition angle.
ratio_height_fast_back (rhfb)
Height ratio of the fastback surface relative to the overall body height. Governs the severity of the rear slope angle and the onset of trailing-edge separation from the roof surface. This parameter has the second-highest importance score at 17.8% behind the rear fastback ratio.
side_taper
Side wall taper angle in degrees. Positive taper narrows the Windsor body toward the rear, reducing the width of the separated wake and typically lowering total drag by reducing the effective base area exposed to low-pressure wake flow.
clearance
Ground clearance in millimetres between the lowest body surface and the ground plane. Affects underbody flow velocity and the balance between base pressure recovery and underbody suction force. Interacts with the bottom taper angle to determine diffuser effectiveness.
bottom_taper_angle
Angle of the underbody diffuser ramp in degrees. Higher angles increase underbody pressure recovery but risk diffuser stall and flow separation above approximately 12 degrees, which reverses the drag benefit. The optimal angle depends on ground clearance and body length.
frontal_area (m squared)
Projected frontal area in square metres at Windsor body scale. Directly scales the pressure drag component according to the definition: Cd equals drag force divided by the product of 0.5 times air density times velocity squared times frontal area. Windsor body values range from approximately 0.080 to 0.120 m squared across the 355 WindsorML samples.
